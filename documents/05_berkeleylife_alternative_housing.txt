Alternative Off-Campus Housing

There are other housing possibilities beyond typical rental search websites and online listings. Here are some alternatives to consider:

Berkeley Student Cooperative

The Berkeley Student Cooperative (Co-ops) is a low-cost, collaborative living community. Each resident must contribute five work shift hours per week, including washing dishes, cleaning the kitchen, cooking, and other household duties.

Members describe the time commitment as manageable. One resident noted that "the work shift commitment is 5 hours, which is honestly less than you would end up working if you are living by yourself." Another mentioned that "cookshift is super sociable and I enjoy it as a way to unwind and chat to friends whilst getting in my hours."

Work shift hours can reduce rental costs further. Students can request rent compensation or paid work shifts if they work additional hours beyond the requirement, such as through Central Food Service positions.

Room assignments operate on a points system. When residents first move in, they occupy a temporary room for a week to meet housemates, then participate in a housing bid based on accumulated points. Members can choose roommates and combine points or squat (keep the same room) in subsequent semesters.

The community is described as intentional about creating spaces for low-income students, people of color, and disabled individuals. One resident stated the experience has been "more friendly than most group living situations" with an emphasis on intentional community building.

International House

International House welcomes both international and U.S. students. Approximately seventy-five percent of residents are international students, while twenty-five percent are from the United States.

Eligibility requires being a registered Berkeley student at junior/senior standing with at least 60 UC credits completed, a graduate or postdoctoral student, visiting scholar, or Education Abroad Reciprocity student.

According to one U.S. resident, "I have really enjoyed living in I-House and have met some of my closest friends at Berkeley here." The residence offers study lounges and dining facilities, though rooms are small and the facility is expensive.

The residence hosts weekly Wednesday coffee hours celebrating different countries and twice-semester Sunday Suppers (formal dinners) free for residents. However, as a privately run facility, students have limited input on administrative decisions, and dining commons hours are restricted.

Greek Housing

Greek chapters often own their properties, providing rent-controlled housing with scholarships available through Panhellenic or Interfraternity Councils. Most chapters require members to live in house for at least one year, with exceptions for study abroad or leave of absence.

Greek housing typically includes meal plans with personal chefs, housekeepers, maintenance workers, and house managers. Students should only consider chapters recognized by the university, as these are the only organizations overseen by university staff and subject to safety and wellness standards. Unrecognized groups do not follow university regulations, potentially putting students at risk.

The Fraternity & Sorority Life office coordinates with the Center for Student Conduct to address misconduct in recognized chapters. A list of recognized and unrecognized chapters is available through the student leadership website.
